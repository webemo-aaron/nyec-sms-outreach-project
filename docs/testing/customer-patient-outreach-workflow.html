Customer and Patient Experience

NYeC SMS Outreach Customer Workflow

This document describes the experience the customer team, outreach operator, support team, and patient should have when running an SMS outreach campaign. It is written as an operating model, not a technical build plan.

Customer Outcome

Prepared campaign

Customer approves cohort, message, sender identity, schedule, and support path.

Operator Outcome

Controlled launch

Operator imports MEF data, reviews rejects, launches, dispatches, and monitors evidence.

Patient Outcome

Clear next step

Patient receives a concise message with sender context, action link, and opt-out language.

Compliance Outcome

Auditable trail

Every configuration, dispatch, callback, opt-out, and reset creates reviewable evidence.

Experience Principles

Minimum Necessary Content

SMS copy should avoid PHI and should not expose diagnosis, program eligibility, or sensitive details.

Consent and Control

Every patient-facing message includes opt-out language, and STOP/START behavior is immediate and visible.

Human Support Path

Customer support should know what campaign is active, what the message says, and how to handle confusion or opt-out requests.

Customer-to-Patient Journey

The outreach process has four operating lanes: customer readiness, operator execution, system processing, and patient response.

Customer Readiness Checklist

  • Confirm the outreach purpose and success metric, such as completed questionnaire, registration, or follow-up response.
  • Confirm the approved cohort source and date, plus who owns corrections for rejected rows.
  • Approve sender identity, message body, support script, daily limit, send window, and launch date.
  • Confirm whether recipients have a support number, landing page, or survey URL that matches the message.
  • Confirm customer support can recognize the campaign name and route patient questions.
  • Confirm opt-out expectations: STOP suppresses future dispatch, START re-enables eligibility.

Patient SMS Experience

The patient should understand who is contacting them, what action is requested, and how to opt out. The message should not require the patient to infer clinical or eligibility details from the SMS. 

Hello Ana, NYC Health Center A asks you to complete a secure questionnaire.
Visit https://survey.example.test/r/10001. Reply STOP to opt out.
Use customer-approved language. Keep sensitive context behind the secure destination rather than in the SMS body.

Operational Roles

Role Responsibilities Evidence They Need
Customer Owner Approves campaign goal, message, cohort, send window, and support process. Campaign summary, MEF accepted/rejected counts, approved SMS copy, launch decision.
Outreach Operator Imports MEF, creates campaign, launches, dispatches, monitors callbacks, resets test state. Dashboard, dispatches, outbound messages, audit events, billing summary, opt-out list.
Support Team Answers patient questions, explains the message, routes technical issues, honors opt-out questions. Active campaign name, sender identity, message copy, destination URL, escalation contact.
Patient Receives SMS, completes requested action, replies STOP or START if desired, contacts support if confused. Clear sender context, secure link, opt-out language, support path if provided by customer.

Customer Handoff Package

  • Campaign name, launch date, send window, daily limit, sender identity, and approved SMS copy.
  • MEF import summary: total rows, accepted rows, rejected rows, and top rejection reasons.
  • Support script: why the patient is receiving the message, what the link does, and who to contact for help.
  • Opt-out behavior: STOP suppresses future messages; START re-enables eligibility if permitted by customer policy.
  • Monitoring plan: dashboard review cadence, callback failure review, and escalation owner.
  • Post-run evidence: dispatch count, delivery status, opt-outs, billing estimate, and audit event export.

Release Readiness From the Customer Perspective

Question Ready Signal Stop Signal
Can the customer explain the campaign? Goal, audience, message, and support flow are documented. Support cannot identify the message or patient next step.
Can the operator prove safe execution? Local validation passed with synthetic data and reset evidence. Dispatch, callback, opt-out, or audit evidence is missing.
Can the patient control future messaging? STOP and START are tested and visible in opt-out review. STOP does not suppress future dispatch eligibility.
Can the team avoid unnecessary credit spend? Twilio test credentials and magic number validate request path before live sender testing. Live credentials are used before local no-credit validation passes.
This workflow intentionally avoids real member data and PHI. Customer-specific policy, consent language, and regulatory review should be completed before live outreach.
